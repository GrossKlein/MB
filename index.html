Bleich's $578 Million Question

Zero Item 11 disclosures. $578 million in institutional assets. One man's regulatory history — invisible to the standard screening process.

Zero. That is the number of disciplinary disclosures on the Form ADV of EntryPoint Capital, LLC — the document that pension funds, endowments, and family offices rely on to evaluate their fund managers. Zero Item 11 entries. Zero regulatory actions. Zero material legal proceedings. A clean sheet.

EntryPoint Capital manages $578 million. Its CEO is Lawrence Leibowitz, former Chief Operating Officer of the New York Stock Exchange. Its administrator is SS&C Technologies. Its strategy — "Third Moment," a reference to statistical skewness — signals quantitative sophistication. The pitch to allocators is impeccable.

But the man who owns the firm and controls the investment decisions is Michael Edward Bleich (CRD# 4262671). And Bleich's regulatory history is not zero. It is $3,000,000 in FINRA fines. It is 295,950,000 phantom shares generated through systematic naked short selling. It is four dissolved corporate entities over thirteen years — each dissolution resetting the disclosure clock so that the next firm's CRD search returns nothing. The gap between what EntryPoint's filings show and what the public record contains is the central finding of this investigation.

Bleich at Lehman: The Making of a Market Structure Operator

Head of Liquidity Strategy for U.S. Equities. BATS board member. A physicist who learned how settlement systems worked — from the inside.

Before Michael Bleich generated 295 million phantom shares, he learned how settlement systems worked from the inside. As Head of Liquidity Strategy for U.S. Equities at Lehman Brothers (2004–2008), he was responsible for algorithmic routing, dark pool utilization, and electronic execution — one of the most powerful positions in electronic trading. He championed Lehman's minority investment in BATS Trading, the Electronic Communication Network that the SEC would later penalize $14 million for creating secretive order types disclosed selectively to elite high-frequency trading firms.

Bleich sat on the BATS board. He held a doctorate in theoretical physics from Duke University (1997). He had entered finance not through the traditional routes of investment banking or sales, but through the mathematics of market microstructure — the study of how orders move through electronic systems, how latency creates arbitrage, how settlement mechanics can be exploited by those who understand them precisely.

On April 8, 2006, he married Alexis Kate Kaminsky at the Palm House of the Brooklyn Botanic Garden. The New York Times ran the announcement. A year later, the couple purchased Unit 1 at 13 Harrison Street in Tribeca for $3.6 million.

Then Lehman Brothers collapsed. And Bleich took what he had learned about the plumbing and built his own machine.

Bleich's Scout Trading Machine: 295 Million Phantom Shares

255 naked redemption orders. 14 leveraged ETFs. Two years. Zero written supervisory procedures. A $3,000,000 fine.

Those are the numbers from FINRA AWC 2010024386601, the enforcement action that ended Scout Trading, LLC — the firm Michael Bleich founded in 2009, one year after Lehman's bankruptcy.

The mechanics were precise. Scout submitted massive ETF redemption orders while "insufficiently long" — industry parlance for not owning the shares being tendered. This triggered automatic fails-to-deliver by the T+3 settlement deadline. Scout then invoked the bona fide market maker exemption, which grants an additional three days to settle. FINRA specifically found that Scout was not a market maker. At the T+6 deadline, Scout closed the position — and immediately re-established an identical naked position the next day.

The cycle repeated continuously from January 2010 to March 2012. In the Direxion Daily Financial Bear 3X Shares ETF alone, Scout's activity accounted for 59 percent of all fails-to-deliver nationwide. One firm, controlled by one individual, had distorted the settlement system for an entire publicly traded security across American markets.

Evidence: Zero Compliance Infrastructure

Scout Trading maintained zero written supervisory procedures throughout the violation period. Not inadequate procedures. Not lax procedures. None. For more than two years, the firm operated a systematic short-selling scheme with no compliance infrastructure of any kind. The absence was not a gap. It was a design choice.

Evidence: The Market Maker Contradiction

CBOE Docket 12-0059 (January 2013) reveals what may be the most damning detail in the enforcement record. Scout Trading was fined $10,000 for failing on "numerous occasions" to provide and maintain two-sided 100-share quotes in its remote market-making assignment — a violation of CBOE Rule 53.23. Providing continuous two-sided liquidity is the defining obligation of a market maker. Scout was claiming market-maker privileges to obtain the T+6 settlement window while simultaneously failing to fulfill the only duty that justified those privileges. The CBOE's own enforcement record proves the exemption was fraudulently invoked.

The CBOE actions did not stop there. Docket 12-0099 (June 2013) found that Scout had failed to designate a Chief Compliance Officer or maintain registration records — a $7,500 fine for running a regulated entity with no designated compliance officer on record. Docket 14-0018 (May 2014) imposed $5,000 for Regulation NMS trade-through violations. Three separate enforcement actions from a single exchange, each documenting a different dimension of the same operational posture: rules as obstacles to be circumvented, not constraints to be honored.

On April 7, 2015, FINRA censured Scout Trading and imposed a $3,000,000 fine — the capstone of a two-year enforcement accumulation. Wedbush Securities, the clearing firm that processed Scout's orders without adequate oversight, would later pay $675,000 in its own penalties.

Between the dissolution of Ophir Partners and the formation of EntryPoint Capital, the record shows Bleich at Inferent Capital (2017–2020) as a Research Team Lead in machine learning and quantitative research — a transitional role at a firm he did not own. The interlude matters less for what Bleich did there than for what was happening in the background: the disclosure clock on his predecessor entities was running down. By the time EntryPoint Capital filed its first Form ADV, the dissolved firms were receding into archival obscurity — findable only by a searcher who already knew to look.

The sequence matters because of what it accomplishes. Each time an entity carrying enforcement baggage is dissolved and a new entity is formed with a clean CRD number, the standard FINRA BrokerCheck and SEC IAPD searches run against the new number. The regulatory history does not transfer. The disclosure clock resets.

Search EntryPoint Capital's CRD — 313946 — and the result is clean. No enforcement actions. No disciplinary proceedings. No material legal events. A compliance officer pulling Bleich's individual BrokerCheck report would find the Scout Trading connection — but only if the search began with Bleich the person, rather than EntryPoint the firm. The distinction is the mechanism. Institutional due diligence teams running entity-level screens will find nothing.

That is how $578 million in institutional capital came to be managed by an individual with $3 million in documented regulatory penalties — without the standard screening process catching it.

EntryPoint Capital's $578 Million Disclosure Gap

Form ADV Item 11: zero entries. The question the SEC's Division of Examinations is positioned to resolve.

EntryPoint Capital's Form ADV identifies Michael Bleich as a control person through his indirect ownership via EntryPoint Partners, LP. The same Form ADV contains zero Item 11 disclosures — the section where disciplinary history, regulatory actions, and material legal proceedings involving the adviser or its control persons are catalogued.

The $3 million FINRA fine was levied against an entity Bleich founded and controlled. Whether the entity dissolution pattern creates a technical basis for non-disclosure — or whether Item 11 requires disclosure of a control person's regulatory history at predecessor entities — is a question the SEC's Division of Examinations is positioned to resolve. What is not in question is the information asymmetry: pension funds, endowments, and family offices allocating capital to EntryPoint's Third Moment strategy are making decisions without access to the complete enforcement record of the individual who controls their capital.

Leibowitz, Bleich, and the Governance Question

Zero percent ownership. Six concurrent board seats. The CEO holds the title. Bleich holds the firm.

Lawrence Leibowitz's credentials are not in question. Former Chief Operating Officer of NYSE Euronext. Head of Global Equities Markets. Managing Director of Americas Equities at UBS. Board positions at Forge Global Holdings, Janus Henderson, and multiple additional entities. He is one of the most credentialed executives in market structure.

The structural question is narrower: what does it mean for a firm's governance when the CEO holds no economic stake in its long-term performance, while the person who does hold beneficial ownership — and who controls the investment strategy — is the individual with the $3 million regulatory history? The Form ADV confirms the arrangement explicitly: Bleich is the indirect beneficial owner through EntryPoint Partners, LP. Leibowitz holds the title. Bleich holds the firm.

This does not establish that Leibowitz is operationally disengaged; he may be deeply involved in daily decisions. But the structural incentives concentrate authority in the owner-CIO, not the non-owner CEO. Allocators must evaluate whether that structure provides a genuine operational firewall or functions primarily as a credentialing layer designed to clear due diligence screens. The documented facts support either reading. The distinction determines whether Leibowitz's presence is a control mechanism or a signal.

Evidence: Reciprocal Governance

When Jose Marques departed EntryPoint in February 2022 to become CEO of Intech Investment Management, Leibowitz assumed the EntryPoint CEO role. Leibowitz was subsequently appointed to Intech's board — the firm where his predecessor now leads. Reciprocal governance arrangements are common in institutional finance, but they invite scrutiny at any firm where the separation of economic interest from operational authority is already a live issue.

One additional structural detail warrants examination. EntryPoint's Form ADV identifies Andrew Alkon as simultaneously holding the roles of Chief Compliance Officer, Chief Operating Officer, and Chief Financial Officer. At a $578 million fund, the compliance function exists to provide independent oversight of operations and finance — the very functions Alkon also runs. The three-role concentration eliminates the structural independence that compliance is designed to provide. For a firm whose founder built and operated Scout Trading with zero written supervisory procedures, the design choice is familiar.

The Bleich Debt Chain: From Corporate Default to Personal Lien

$2,233,007. A promissory note at 10% interest. A default at 15%. A UCC-1 lien on the family home in Tribeca.

$2,233,007. That is the principal on the promissory note Michael and Alexis Bleich signed to OPEH LLC on January 11, 2018 — bearing interest at 10 percent per annum, compounded annually, with a default rate of 15 percent. The note was secured by a UCC-1 lien on their 13 Harrison Street residence — the same Tribeca condo purchased for $3.6 million in 2007.

The note's origin is the key. It was tendered "in connection with the satisfaction and discharge of the corporate obligation of Ophir Partners, LLC." The Bleiches personally assumed more than $2.2 million in debt that belonged to their defunct hedge fund — the entity dissolved because Scout Trading's regulatory contagion had made continued SEC registration untenable.

They defaulted on September 1, 2018. Accrued interest: $155,070. OPEH LLC filed suit (Index No. 654791/2018) seeking $2,388,077. The case was discontinued by stipulation in December 2018, but the underlying obligation — compounding at the default rate — has not been publicly resolved.

The chain is direct: $3 million enforcement action against Scout → dissolution of Scout → withdrawal of Ophir → personal assumption of Ophir's corporate debt → default and UCC-1 lien. The regulatory history is not abstract. It has a dollar amount, a default date, and a lien on the family home.

For institutional investors, this chain exposes a dimension of risk that no Form ADV captures. The CIO of a $578 million fund carries personal financial obligations arising from predecessor entity failure — obligations that trace directly to the same enforcement actions that his current firm's filings do not disclose. The undisclosed history and the personal financial distress are two faces of the same fact.

Bleich's Lawyers: Fabrication Findings and Suspended Licenses

$198,877.50 in anti-SLAPP sanctions. 26 documented false statements. A suspended co-counsel. A PHV-ineligible lead litigator.

$198,877.50. That is the anti-SLAPP fee sanction entered against the client of Robert A. Giacovas in Medifast v. Minkow (S.D. Cal. 3:10-cv-00382) — the cost of defending against claims a federal court found were built on fabricated quotes. Judge Janis Sammartino ruled that Giacovas "paraphrased statements seemingly for maximum shock value." The court granted the defendants' anti-SLAPP motions and dismissed all claims. After the ruling, forensic accountant Tracy Coenen documented 26 specific instances of false statements in Giacovas's appellate briefs.

Giacovas is Michael Bleich's lead litigator. He represents the Bleiches in their current property dispute (Index No. 155721/2024) through his firm, Lazare Potter Giacovas & Moyle LLP. In February 2026, the New Jersey Lawyers' Fund for Client Protection confirmed him ineligible for pro hac vice admission.

His co-counsel is Lynn Ellen Judell of Rukab Brash PLLC (California Bar #114924). Judell's California Bar license has been suspended since July 2, 2024. The State Bar has issued a formal Consumer Alert regarding her status. She carries a prior 2006 administrative suspension. She appeared as co-counsel on the June 21, 2024 memorandum of law in Bleich's preliminary injunction motion — eleven days before her suspension became effective. She is also listed on the New Jersey PHV ineligibility list.

Evidence: Pattern of Litigation Tactics

The pattern of Giacovas's tactics in the Bleich litigation tracks the Medifast playbook: fabrication of material facts as foundation, procedural attrition to inflate costs, equitable arguments that conceal the client's own violations, and settlement demands designed to extract concessions under economic pressure. In the Bleich matter, the verified complaint allegedly pleads ownership of Floor Area Ratio rights — a zoning concept the underlying condominium declaration does not assign to individual unit owners — while concealing the Bleiches' own Certificate of Occupancy violations in their cellar unit.

A client with a documented history of exploiting structural loopholes chose attorneys with a documented history of fabricating material facts. The alignment is precise, and it is visible to any tribunal that looks.

The Gap

Standard entity-level CRD screening of EntryPoint Capital (CRD# 313946) would not have surfaced the $3,000,000 FINRA enforcement action against Scout Trading (CRD# 151990). It would not have identified the four-entity dissolution sequence. It would not have revealed the $2,233,007 OPEH default, the 55.6 percent non-reportable capital ratio, or the licensing status of the firm's litigation counsel. Each of these findings required searches that began with Bleich the individual — not EntryPoint the firm — and then traced backward through predecessor CRD numbers, NYSCEF court indices, state bar records, and CBOE docket archives.

An institutional allocator relying on Form ADV Item 11 disclosures, entity-level BrokerCheck results, and standard compliance questionnaires would have encountered none of this information. The disclosure gap is not a hypothesis. It is a structural feature of the entity dissolution pattern — and $578 million in institutional capital currently sits on one side of it.
